Research indicates that the type and nature of the traumatic experience play a significant role in determining the likelihood of developing PTSD234. A World Health Organization analysis of trauma exposure revealed that witnessing death or serious injury, and the unexpected death of a loved one, were linked to the highest proportion of PTSD cases3. While the average risk of developing PTSD after trauma exposure is around 4%, it can reach up to 30% depending on the specific type of trauma2346.

While combat and violence are often associated with PTSD, it's crucial to recognize that trauma can stem from various sources78910. Major health conditions, motor vehicle accidents, health pandemics (e.g., COVID-19), and acting as a first responder in emergencies are just a few examples of potentially traumatic experiences that may lead to PTSD7891011. A meta-analysis of the COVID-19 pandemic, for instance, reported a global PTSD prevalence of 17.52% among the general population12. Notably, healthcare professionals faced a significantly higher risk, with 30.98% experiencing PTSD compared to 15.45% of COVID-19 patients/survivors12.

Beyond the nature of the traumatic event itself, certain individual characteristics can also influence the likelihood of developing PTSD5613. This includes:

Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity

Research suggests that LGBTQ+ individuals are at an increased risk of developing PTSD compared to heterosexual individuals14. This may be due to experiences of discrimination, violence, and social stigma14.

Race

Studies have shown that Black individuals face a higher risk of developing PTSD compared to White individuals15. This disparity may be linked to systemic racism, exposure to community violence, and historical trauma15.
