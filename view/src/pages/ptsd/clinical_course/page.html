Clinical Course

PTSD symptoms may occur soon after index trauma or may be delayed3.

Delayed onset PTSD is defined as meeting diagnostic criteria ≥6 months after the index event1. PTSD onset is usually within 3 months of the trauma2.

Individuals may not meet full criteria for PTSD for months after the trauma2. Some symptoms may appear immediately but with a delay in meeting the full criteria (subsyndromal PTSD), or individuals can have a completely symptom free period2.

At least 25% of individuals with PTSD experience delayed onset3, but this could be higher as one study found that 37.3% of individuals who had PTSD at 12 months post-trauma did not have PTSD at 3 months post-trauma3.

The symptoms of PTSD and the relative predominance of different symptoms may vary over time, as can duration of symptoms2.

It is important to understand delayed-onset PTSD has consequences for follow-up and diagnosis of trauma victims4.

Factors affecting delayed onset

Military combat exposure, Western cultural background, and lower cumulative PTSD incidence (with initial subthreshold symptoms) have been associated with delayed-onset PTSD5

Multiple studies have found that there is a higher rate of delayed-onset PTSD in military populations compared to civilian populations6. There are multiple hypotheses as to why, including emotional numbing and the effect of multiple tours6.

Frequency of delayed-onset PTSD in civilians and military6

PTSD is considered chronic once symptoms have been present for more than 3 months7

1/3 of patients may recover by one-year follow-up8

More than 1/3 remain symptomatic 10 years after index trauma8

PTSD symptom recurrence and intensification may occur in response to reminders of the original trauma, ongoing life stressors, or newly experienced traumatic events2

Declining health, worsening cognitive functioning, and social isolation may exacerbate symptoms in the elderly2

The symptoms of PTSD and the relative predominance of different symptoms may vary over time, as can duration of symptoms2

Individuals with PTSD may experience significant delays in diagnosis

≈50% of patients with PTSD seek treatment91011

Average time from index trauma to symptom presentation: 2.2 years12

Average time from index trauma to PTSD diagnosis: 8.7 years12

Median time from onset of symptoms to treatment is 12 years911

Many patients and individuals with PTSD seek care for physical symptoms without mentioning psychiatric symptoms or trauma histories due to a lack of understanding regarding the relationship between trauma exposure and their own symptoms13

PTSD is often underdiagnosed or misdiagnosed as other mental health conditions

Most prominent barriers to care14

  • Concerns related to stigma, shame/rejection
  • Low mental health literacy
  • Lack of knowledge and treatment-related doubts
  • Fear of negative social consequences
  • Limited resources

Individuals with PTSD are 80% more likely than those without PTSD to have symptoms that meet diagnostic criteria for at least one other mental health disorder (e.g., depressive, bipolar, anxiety, or substance use disorders)2.

Misdiagnosis
Underdiagnosis

In the primary care setting, of individuals meeting diagnostic criteria for PTSD15:

  • 50% were diagnosed with depression
  • 23% were diagnosed with anxiety or panic attacks
  • 11% received a diagnosis of PTSD

<50% of individuals who meet criteria for PTSD are correctly diagnosed in primary and secondary care settings131617

Decreased awareness in the general population and underuse of existing diagnostic tools

Misdiagnosis is associated with ineffective management, leading to negative impact on20:

  • Treatment compliance
  • Treatment response
  • Patient satisfaction

PTSD diagnostic screening tools are underused in practice2122

Only 7% trauma centres screen for PTSD23. The gold-standard test for PTSD is considered to be the CAPS-5 test, but this is a time-consuming test that requires training23.

PTSD screening tools can aid in provisional diagnosis, especially for physicians with minimal training in PTSD diagnosis, and make it easier to diagnose PTSD without having to assess all PTSD symptoms, which can be time consuming2122. Two screening tools that are psychometrically sound and aid in the probable diagnosis of PTSD: Primary Care PTSD Screen for DSM-5 (PC-PTSD-5)21and PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5)22. These tools are recommended in guidelines based on a systematic evidence-based review by the US Department of Veterans Affairs and the Department of Defense24
