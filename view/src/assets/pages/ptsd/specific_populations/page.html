PTSD in Women

In the general population, women are 2x as likely as men to develop PTSD, partly due to their increased exposure to traumatic events closely associated with PTSD, such as sexual abuse and rape1. Women account for 66.4% of the overall PTSD population in the US1.

Even after adjusting for differences in trauma exposure and prior victimization or abuse history, women still exhibit a significantly elevated risk of developing PTSD compared to men, with a lifetime prevalence of PTSD of 13% in women and 6% in men, suggesting a higher susceptibility among women6. Generic research suggests a greater heritability risk in women, with genes like adenylate cyclase activating polypeptide 1 (pituitary) receptor (ADCYAP1R1) showing allelic variations linked to PTSD risk1. Ultimately, the heightened prevalence of PTSD among women likely arises from a combination of increased trauma exposure and inherent vulnerabilities1. Additionally, females in the general population experience PTSD for a longer duration than males5.

Gender differences
PTSD diagnoses among military personnel vary based on gender, with the prevalence of PTSD higher among female service members4. Male veterans reported higher levels of war zone exposure, while female veterans reported experiencing interpersonal violence and military sex trauma (MST)46.
Peaks in military PTSD incidence over the years
Increase in PTSD cases align with times of heightened military engagement, such as deployments following the events of 9/116. Among the 2.7 million personnel deployed to Iraq or Afghanistan since 2001, it's estimated that 5-20% have experienced PTSD7.

LGBTQ+

Up to 48% among lesbian, gay and bisexual individuals9
Up to 48% among transgender and gender diverse individuals9
Heightened exposure to trauma
The LGBTQ+ community faces a disproportionately high risk of trauma and PTSD compared to cisgender/heterosexual individuals, primarily due to increased exposure to hate crimes, intimate partner violence, and sexual assaults10
Childhood abuse
Childhood abuse is more prevalent among sexual minority children, significantly contributing to mental health disparities, particularly in PTSD, and accounting for up to half of the disparities in PTSD based on sexual orientation1011
Internalized stigma
Research suggests that internalized homophobia (IH) predicts the severity of PTSD symptoms in LGBTQ+ minorities with trauma histories10.IH in sexual minorities correlates with depression, substance use, and low self-esteem, and reduces the likelihood of recovery from traumatic events such as sexual assault and hate crimes12.
Social stressors
LGBTQ individuals experience elevated levels of discrimination, victimization, and minority stress. These social stressors often prompt feelings of shame and the concealment of their minority identity, contributing to heightened mental and physical health challenges within the LGBTQ+ community9.

Race

Race-PTSD prevalence

  • Black individuals experience more frequent, severe, and chronic PTSD symptoms compared to White individuals14.
  • Despite higher rates of PTSD and trauma exposure among low-income Black individuals, racial disparities persist even when controlling for these factors14.
  • Racial discrimination triggers negative emotional states and heightened stress responses, increasing vulnerability to mental health difficulties like depression, anxiety, and substance use14.
  • Chronic exposure to racial discrimination exacerbates mental health disparities in Black populations and has been associated with increased severity of PTSD symptoms1415.
