Specific Populations

Civilian / General Population

The global lifetime prevalence of PTSD is 4-10% [1][2].

PTSD is one of the most common mental disorders in the US, with 7-8 out of every 100 people experiencing PTSD at some point in their lifetime [3][4].

~13 million adults in the US will experience PTSD during a given year [5].

>80% of PTSD patients are in the general population rather than the military population [6].

The typical age at which PTSD first appears is during young and middle adulthood. Among adults in the United States, the median age of onset if around 23 years old [7].

The majority of individuals exposed to trauma do not develop PTSD [1]. However, the type of trauma experienced influences the likelihood of developing PTSD. For more information click here

PTSD in Women

In the general population, women are 2x as likely as men to develop PTSD, partly due to their increased exposure to traumatic events closely associated with PTSD, such as sexual abuse and rape [1]. Women account for 66.4% of the overall PTSD population in the US [6].

Even after adjusting for differences in trauma exposure and prior victimization or abuse history, women still exhibit a significantly elevated risk of developing PTSD compared to men, with a lifetime prevalence of PTSD of 13% in women and 6% in men, suggesting a higher susceptibility among women [6]. Generic research suggests a greater heritability risk in women, with genes like adenylate cyclase activating polypeptide 1 (pituitary) receptor (ADCYAP1R1) showing allelic variations linked to PTSD risk [1]. Ultimately, the heightened prevalence of PTSD among women likely arises from a combination of increased trauma exposure and inherent vulnerabilities [1]. Additionally, females in the general population experience PTSD for a longer duration than males [5].

Military population

PTSD prevalence within the military [4]

Heighted exposure to trauma

PTSD is a significant issue among U.S military veterans, often stemming from exposure to traumatic events during service [4][6]

Gender differences

PTSD diagnoses among military personnel vary based on gender, with the prevalence of PTSD higher among female service members [4]. Male veterans reported higher levels of war zone exposure, while female veterans reported experiencing interpersonal violence and military sex trauma (MST) [4][6].

Peaks in military PTSD incidence over the years

Increase in PTSD cases align with times of heightened military engagement, such as deployments following the events of 9/11 [6]. Among the 2.7 million personnel depoled to Iraq or Afghanistan since 2001, it's estimated that 5-20% have experienced PTSD [7].

LGBTQ+ and maginalized groups

PTSD prevalence within the LGBTQ community

Up to 48% among lesbian, gay and bisexual individuals [9]

Up to 42% among transgender and gender diverse individuals [9].



Heightened exposure to trauma

The LGBTQ+ community faces a disproportionately high risk of trauma and PTSD compared to cisgender/heterosexual individuals, primarily due to increased exposure to hate crimes, intimate partner violence, and sexual assaults [10]

Childhood abuse

Childhood abuse is more prevalent among sexual minority children, significantly contributing to mental health disparities, particularly in PTSD, and accounting for up to half of the disparities in PTSD based on sexual orientation [10] [11].

Internalized stigma

Research suggests that internalized homophobia (IH) predicts the severity of PTSD symptoms in LGBTQ+ minorities with trauma histories [10]. IH in sexual minorities correlates with depression, substance use, and low self-esteem, and reduces the likelihood of recovery from traumatic events such as sexual assault and hate crimes [12]

Social stressors

LGBTQ individuals experience elevated levels of discrimination, victimization, and minority stress. These social stressors often prompt feelings of shame and the concealment of their minority identity, contributing to heightened mental and physical health challenges within the LGBTQ+ community [9].

Race

PTSD prevalence [13]: Black: 8.7%; White: 7.4%; Hispanic: 7.0%; Asian: 4.0%

  • Black individuals experience more frequent, severe, and chronic PTSD symptoms compared to White individuals [14].

  • Despite higher rates of PTSD and trauma exposure among low-income Black individuals, racial disparities persist even when controlling for these factors [14].

  • Racial discrimination triggers negative emotional states and heightened stress responses, increasing vulnerability to mental health difficulties like depression, anxiety, and substance use [14].

  • Chronic exposure to racial discrimination exacerbates mental health disparities in Black populations and has been associated with increased severity of PTSD symptoms [14] [15].

Increased risk of PTSD is associated with a number of demographic and behavioral factors [16] [5]:
